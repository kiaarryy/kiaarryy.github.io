    <h2>AutoModelling: BMS data becomes validated Modelica</h2>
    <p class="feature-lead">A reproducible pipeline now carries raw building-management data all the way to executable, quantitatively evaluated chiller models—without hiding missing points, weak steady-state coverage, or non-compliant outputs.</p>
    <p class="feature-result"><strong>98.5%</strong> less modelling effort at the most demanding case-study site.</p>
    <details class="story-details">
      <summary>Learn more <span aria-hidden="true">↘</span></summary>
      <div class="story-details__body">
        <p>The study screened 22 chillers across a data centre, commercial complex, and university campus. Fifteen contained enough complete, steady-state data for modelling; nine satisfied both the adopted power and COP criteria.</p>
        <p>The contribution is not a single fitted equation but a transparent workflow: semantic mapping, preprocessing, steady-state detection, model-family selection, Modelica generation, OpenModelica execution, and validation.</p>
        <p><a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2026.141700">Paper DOI</a></p>

    <h2>39,202 atrium designs, compressed into real-time insight</h2>
    <p class="feature-lead">The new meta-sampling method preserves the evidence of a full parametric simulation library while making sensitivity analysis and early-stage energy exploration fast enough for design practice.</p>
    <p class="feature-result"><strong>87.6%</strong> computational savings with no more than <strong>0.35%</strong> output error.</p>
    <details class="story-details">
      <summary>Learn more <span aria-hidden="true">↘</span></summary>
      <div class="story-details__body">
        <p>Eight atrium parameters were evaluated against heating, cooling, and lighting energy. Atrium area and internal openness emerged as the strongest controllable factors; orientation created consistent differences between south- and west-facing designs.</p>
        <p>Multi-objective optimization identified configurations with up to 40.29% lower annual total energy use.</p>
        <p><a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2025.137501">Paper DOI</a></p>
      </div>

    <h2>Thermal-response regimes found inside large public atria</h2>
    <p class="feature-lead">A clustering-and-regression method distinguishes recurring thermal states and shows that transitional and traditional atria do not respond to outdoor conditions in the same way.</p>
    <p class="feature-result"><strong>33.8%</strong> higher R² and <strong>62.11%</strong> lower residual sum of squares than conventional analysis.</p>
    <details class="story-details">
      <summary>Learn more <span aria-hidden="true">↘</span></summary>
      <div class="story-details__body">
        <p>The transitional atrium followed outdoor temperature closely (R² = 0.9555), with 30°C marking a critical condition for extended indoor heat retention. The traditional atrium responded more strongly to solar radiation and changed more rapidly.</p>
        <p>The result supports atrium-specific shading, ventilation, envelope, and control strategies rather than one generic large-space prescription.</p>
        <p><a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enbuild.2024.115175">Paper DOI</a></p>
      </div>
    </details>
